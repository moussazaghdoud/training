## 1. The Enterprise Buying Committee

![video: Understanding the Enterprise Buying Committee for UCaaS Deals](sls-buying-committee-overview)

### 1.1 Why You Must Sell to a Committee, Not a Person

In enterprise sales, no single individual makes the buying decision alone. Communications platform purchases are cross-functional decisions that affect IT operations, executive strategy, department productivity, and procurement budgets. If you focus on a single contact, you will lose the deal when an unseen stakeholder raises an objection you never anticipated.

Rainbow deals typically involve a buying committee of **five key personas**. Your job is to identify all five, understand what each cares about, and ensure that each persona has a reason to say yes.

> **Key Concept:** The number-one reason enterprise deals stall is that the salesperson failed to identify all members of the buying committee. Before you invest significant time in a deal, map the committee. If you cannot name at least three of the five personas, you do not yet have enough access to win.

### 1.2 The Five Personas

**Persona 1: IT Director (Technical Evaluator)**

The IT Director is usually your first point of contact and your technical gatekeeper. This person will evaluate Rainbow from an implementation and operations perspective.

- **Role in the decision**: Evaluates technical feasibility, runs proof-of-concept, and provides a recommendation to the CIO
- **Reports to**: CIO or CTO
- **Day-to-day concerns**: System uptime, integration complexity, deployment timelines, security posture, vendor support quality
- **What keeps them up at night**: A failed deployment that disrupts business operations and reflects poorly on their judgment
- **Decision power**: Can veto a solution on technical grounds; rarely has final budget authority

**How to engage the IT Director:**

The IT Director wants details, not marketing slides. Come prepared with architecture diagrams, integration documentation, deployment timelines, and reference customers. Be ready to discuss Active Directory synchronization, SIP trunk compatibility, firewall requirements, and client deployment via SCCM or MDM.

Show respect for their existing infrastructure. Never suggest that their current setup is outdated or inadequate. Instead, position Rainbow as an enhancement: "Your OmniPCX is doing exactly what it should. Rainbow adds a cloud collaboration layer on top without changing anything underneath."

**Persona 2: CIO / CTO (Strategic Decision-Maker)**

The CIO or CTO is the executive sponsor for technology initiatives. They think in terms of strategic direction, not individual product features.

- **Role in the decision**: Sets the technology vision, approves the project, and champions it to the C-suite
- **Reports to**: CEO or Board
- **Day-to-day concerns**: Vendor consolidation, digital transformation roadmap, risk management, competitive advantage through technology
- **What keeps them up at night**: Being locked into a vendor that limits future flexibility, or missing a technology wave that competitors exploit
- **Decision power**: Can approve the project and allocate budget; may delegate technical evaluation to the IT Director

**How to engage the CIO / CTO:**

Speak in strategic terms. Do not lead with features — lead with outcomes. "Rainbow consolidates your communications stack into a single platform with a single vendor, reducing complexity and giving your teams a modern collaboration experience while protecting your existing telephony investment."

Emphasize Rainbow's roadmap and ALE's long-term vision. CIOs plan in 3-5 year horizons. They need to know that the platform they choose today will still be the right choice in 2029.

**Persona 3: CFO (Budget Authority)**

The CFO controls the budget. Even if the CIO wants the project, the CFO can delay or kill it by withholding funding.

- **Role in the decision**: Approves budget, evaluates ROI, and scrutinizes total cost of ownership
- **Reports to**: CEO or Board
- **Day-to-day concerns**: Cost reduction, predictable spending, risk mitigation, compliance with financial controls
- **What keeps them up at night**: Unexpected cost overruns, hidden licensing fees, or a failed project that wasted budget
- **Decision power**: Final budget authority; can approve or reject the financial case regardless of technical merit

**How to engage the CFO:**

The CFO does not care about AI transcription or WebRTC codecs. The CFO cares about three things: how much does it cost, what does it replace, and what is the payback period.

Build a financial model that shows:
- **Current state costs**: PBX maintenance contracts, legacy conferencing tools, multiple point solutions
- **Rainbow costs**: Subscription licensing, implementation services, training
- **Savings**: Eliminated tools, reduced maintenance, avoided PBX replacement costs
- **Net impact**: TCO comparison over 3 and 5 years

The most powerful CFO argument for Rainbow is the **avoided cost of PBX replacement**. If the alternative to Rainbow is a $2 million PBX migration, Rainbow's subscription cost looks modest by comparison.

**Persona 4: Line-of-Business (LOB) Owner (User Champion)**

The LOB Owner is the person who runs a department or business unit that will actually use Rainbow every day. This could be a VP of Sales, Director of Customer Service, Head of Operations, or similar.

- **Role in the decision**: Represents the end-user perspective, validates that the solution solves real business problems
- **Reports to**: C-suite executive in their functional area
- **Day-to-day concerns**: Team productivity, cross-department collaboration, customer responsiveness, tool fatigue
- **What keeps them up at night**: Their team wasting time switching between disconnected tools, or missing customer requests because of communication gaps
- **Decision power**: Can champion or block adoption within their organization; user adoption depends on their buy-in

**How to engage the LOB Owner:**

Show them what their team's day looks like with Rainbow versus without it. Use a scenario: "Right now your sales team switches between email, a separate conferencing tool, a chat app, and the CRM. With Rainbow, they have messaging, calling, conferencing, and CRM integration in one window. That saves 45 minutes per person per day."

LOB Owners respond to demos, not datasheets. Get the product in front of them early.

**Persona 5: Procurement (Commercial Terms)**

Procurement is the final gatekeeper. Once the technical and business cases are approved, Procurement negotiates commercial terms.

- **Role in the decision**: Negotiates pricing, contract terms, SLAs, and legal language
- **Reports to**: CFO or Chief Procurement Officer
- **Day-to-day concerns**: Getting the best deal, managing vendor risk, ensuring contractual protections
- **What keeps them up at night**: Signing a contract with hidden escalation clauses or weak exit terms
- **Decision power**: Can delay or modify the deal on commercial grounds; rarely overrides a technical/business decision entirely

**How to engage Procurement:**

Be transparent on pricing from the start. Procurement professionals despise discovering hidden costs late in the process. Provide clear per-user-per-month pricing, explain what is included versus optional, and outline the deal registration and quoting process.

Come prepared with standard contract terms, SLA documentation, and data processing agreements. The faster you satisfy Procurement's checklist, the faster the deal closes.

> **Tip:** Create a stakeholder map for every deal worth more than 50 seats. Draw a simple grid: name, title, role in decision (evaluator, influencer, decision-maker, blocker), attitude toward Rainbow (champion, neutral, skeptic), and what they care about most. Update this map weekly.

## 2. Pain Points by Persona

### 2.1 Mapping Pain to Persona

Understanding pain points is not about memorizing a list — it is about recognizing patterns in the conversations you have. Each persona experiences communications problems differently because they see the world through a different lens.

| Pain Point                         | How Rainbow Addresses It                                                    |
|------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Integration complexity             | Native PBX overlay; no forklift replacement; LDAP/AD sync built in         |
| Too many tools to manage           | Single platform replaces separate chat, conferencing, and telephony tools   |
| Deployment difficulty              | MSI/MDM-deployable clients; admin portal for centralized management        |

1. "What is your 3-year technology vision for internal and external communications?"
2. "How important is reducing the number of vendors in your communications stack?"
3. "Do you have data residency requirements driven by regulation or board policy?"
4. "Where does communications technology fit in your digital transformation roadmap?"

### 2.4 CFO Pain Points

| Pain Point                          | How Rainbow Addresses It                                                   |
|-------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Unpredictable communications costs  | Per-user-per-month subscription pricing with no hidden fees                |
| High total cost of ownership        | Avoids PBX replacement costs; consolidates multiple tool licenses          |

**Discovery questions for the CFO:**

1. "What are you currently spending annually on communications tools — telephony, conferencing, chat?"
2. "Is your PBX maintenance contract increasing year over year?"
3. "If you had to replace your PBX today, what would that cost?"
4. "How do you measure the ROI of collaboration technology investments?"
5. "What is your preferred contract structure — annual, multi-year, or month-to-month?"

### 2.5 LOB Owner Pain Points

| Pain Point                          | How Rainbow Addresses It                                                   |
|-------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Tool fatigue / too many apps        | One platform for messaging, calling, conferencing, and integrations        |
| Poor cross-department collaboration | Bubbles (group spaces) bring together people from different departments    |
| Slow customer response times        | Real-time presence, instant messaging, CRM integration                     |
| Lack of mobile productivity         | Full-featured iOS and Android clients with push notifications              |
| No visibility into team activity    | Admin analytics and presence management                                    |

**Discovery questions for the LOB Owner:**

1. "How does your team communicate with each other today — and are they happy with it?"
2. "How much time do your people spend switching between different communication tools?"
3. "Are there cross-department workflows that break down because of communication gaps?"
4. "Do your team members work from mobile devices, or primarily from desktops?"
5. "If you could change one thing about your team's communication, what would it be?"

> **Warning:** Never assume you know a buyer's pain points before they tell you. Your research gives you hypotheses — the discovery call confirms or corrects them. Asking "I imagine your biggest challenge is X" is presumptuous. Asking "What is your biggest challenge with communications today?" is consultative.

## 3. Buying Signals and Trigger Events

### 3.1 What Is a Buying Signal?

A buying signal is an observable event or statement that indicates a prospect is moving from passive interest to active evaluation. Recognizing buying signals allows you to prioritize your pipeline and focus your energy on deals that are most likely to close.

### 3.2 The Four Primary Buying Signals for Rainbow

**Signal 1: RFP or RFI Issued**

When a prospect issues a formal Request for Proposal (RFP) or Request for Information (RFI) for unified communications, cloud telephony, or collaboration platforms, they are already in an active buying process. This is the strongest buying signal because it means budget has been approved (or is being justified), a timeline exists, and a decision-making process is underway.

**How to respond**: Engage immediately. RFPs often have tight response windows (2-4 weeks). Work with your pre-sales team to tailor the response to the prospect's stated requirements. If you can identify the RFP authors before the response deadline, try to get a meeting to understand the context behind the written requirements.

**Signal 2: PBX End-of-Life or Maintenance Expiration**

When a customer's PBX system reaches end of life, or when their maintenance contract expires and renewal costs spike, they are forced to make a decision: pay more for aging infrastructure, or modernize. This creates a natural window for Rainbow.

**How to respond**: This is where Rainbow's hybrid cloud story is most powerful. The customer does not have to abandon their PBX investment — they can extend its life by overlaying Rainbow. Position Rainbow as the "bridge" that lets them extract more value from their existing PBX while gaining cloud capabilities.

> **Info:** ALE can provide installed base data for OmniPCX Enterprise and OmniPCX Office systems nearing end of life or maintenance renewal. Ask your ALE account team for this data — it is one of the most valuable prospecting tools available to you.

**Signal 3: Compliance Audit or Regulatory Change**

When a customer undergoes a compliance audit (SOC 2, ISO 27001, GDPR review, HIPAA assessment) or faces a new regulatory requirement (NIS2, DORA, national data residency laws), their existing communications tools are often flagged as non-compliant. This creates urgency.

**How to respond**: Lead with data sovereignty and security certifications. Show the customer exactly which data center their data would reside in, provide the SOC 2 Type II report (under NDA), and walk through the data processing agreement. Make it easy for them to check the compliance box.

**Signal 4: Microsoft Teams Deployment (or Dissatisfaction)**

This may seem counterintuitive, but a Teams deployment is actually a buying signal for Rainbow. Why? Because Teams deployments frequently expose gaps:

- Teams cannot integrate with the existing PBX
- Teams data is processed on US-controlled infrastructure, creating sovereignty concerns
- Teams telephony (Phone System) is expensive and complex to deploy
- Users complain about Teams fatigue — too many notifications, too many channels

When a prospect mentions frustration with Teams, that is your opening. Rainbow can coexist with Teams (users can run both), or it can replace the telephony and collaboration layer while leaving Teams as the document collaboration tool.

### 3.3 How to Detect Buying Signals

Buying signals do not always arrive as explicit statements. Train yourself to listen for indirect indicators:

| What You Hear                                            | What It Signals                                          |
|----------------------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------|
| "Our PBX vendor just raised maintenance prices by 20%."  | PBX end-of-life signal                                   |
| "We failed our last compliance audit on data residency." | Compliance trigger                                       |
| "We deployed Teams but our phone system still runs separately." | Teams gap — PBX integration need                  |
| "We are consolidating vendors across the organization."  | Strategic platform evaluation underway                   |
| "Our CEO wants everything in the cloud by next year."    | Top-down digital transformation mandate                  |
| "We have a custom app that needs to send notifications." | CPaaS need — Rainbow Hub opportunity                     |

## 4. MEDDIC Qualification Framework

### 4.1 What Is MEDDIC?

MEDDIC is an enterprise sales qualification methodology that helps you systematically assess whether a deal is real, winnable, and worth pursuing. It was developed at PTC in the 1990s and has become the standard qualification framework in B2B technology sales.

MEDDIC stands for:

- **M** — Metrics
- **E** — Economic Buyer
- **D** — Decision Criteria
- **D** — Decision Process
- **I** — Identify Pain
- **C** — Champion

### 4.2 Applying MEDDIC to Rainbow Deals

**M — Metrics: What measurable outcomes does the buyer expect?**

Metrics are the quantifiable results the customer expects from adopting Rainbow. Without metrics, you cannot build a business case, and without a business case, the CFO will not approve the budget.

Examples of Rainbow-relevant metrics:
- Reduce communications tool costs by 30%
- Avoid $1.5 million PBX replacement cost
- Improve meeting productivity by 20% through AI summaries
- Reduce time-to-resolution for customer inquiries by 15%
- Consolidate from 5 communication tools to 1

**E — Economic Buyer: Who has the budget authority?**

The Economic Buyer is the person who can write the check. In Rainbow deals, this is almost always the **CFO** or a senior VP with delegated budget authority. The CIO may sponsor the project, but the CFO controls the funds.

If you cannot identify and access the Economic Buyer, your deal is at risk. Technical evaluations that never reach the budget holder die quietly.

**D — Decision Criteria: What factors will determine the winner?**

Decision Criteria are the specific factors the buying committee will use to evaluate competing solutions. These typically include:

- Technical requirements (PBX integration, security certifications, data residency)
- Financial requirements (TCO over 3-5 years, licensing model, payment terms)
- Vendor requirements (company stability, support model, references)
- User experience requirements (ease of use, mobile experience, adoption difficulty)

Your job is to discover the criteria and then shape them in Rainbow's favor. If "PBX overlay" is not a criterion and it should be, help the buyer see why it matters.

**D — Decision Process: How will the decision be made?**

The Decision Process is the sequence of steps the organization will follow to reach a decision. Understanding this prevents surprises.

Typical enterprise decision process:
1. Initial research and vendor shortlisting (IT Director leads)
2. RFP or structured evaluation (IT Director + Procurement)
3. Proof of concept or pilot (IT Director + LOB Owner)
4. Business case presentation to C-suite (CIO presents to CFO/CEO)
5. Contract negotiation (Procurement leads)
6. Final approval (Economic Buyer signs)

Ask the buyer: "Can you walk me through your decision process and timeline?" If they cannot answer this question clearly, the decision process is undefined — which is a risk.

**I — Identify Pain: What specific problems are driving the evaluation?**

Pain is the fuel that drives deals forward. Without pain, there is no urgency. Without urgency, deals slip quarter after quarter.

Use the persona-specific pain points from Section 2 to probe for pain during discovery calls. Listen for emotional language: "frustrated," "concerned," "struggling," "wasting time," "falling behind." These words indicate real pain, not hypothetical interest.

**C — Champion: Who inside the organization is actively promoting Rainbow?**

The Champion is an internal advocate who wants Rainbow to win and is willing to spend their political capital to make it happen. The Champion is not just a friendly contact — they are someone with influence and motivation.

A strong Champion:
- Has access to the Economic Buyer
- Understands the internal politics and can navigate them
- Will share insider information about competitive dynamics
- Will coach you on how to position Rainbow for their organization
- Has personal motivation to see the project succeed (e.g., it is their initiative)

If you do not have a Champion, find one. If you cannot find one, the deal is unlikely to close.

### 4.3 MEDDIC Scorecard

Use this scorecard to assess every active deal in your pipeline:

| MEDDIC Element   | Status        | Score | Notes                                    |
|------------------|---------------|-------|------------------------------------------|
| Metrics          | Defined / Not | 0-2   | What measurable outcomes has buyer stated?|
| Economic Buyer   | Identified / Accessed / Not | 0-2 | Can you reach the budget holder?   |
| Decision Criteria| Known / Shaped / Unknown | 0-2  | Do criteria favor Rainbow?              |
| Decision Process | Mapped / Unclear | 0-2  | Do you know every step and timeline?     |
| Identify Pain    | Confirmed / Assumed | 0-2 | Is pain real, urgent, and funded?        |
| Champion         | Strong / Weak / None | 0-2 | Is someone actively selling for you?     |

**Total score: 0-12**
- **10-12**: Strong deal — prioritize and push for close
- **7-9**: Promising deal — address gaps in your MEDDIC coverage
- **4-6**: Risky deal — invest cautiously until gaps are filled
- **0-3**: Unlikely deal — deprioritize or disqualify

## 5. Building Your Discovery Playbook

### 5.1 The First Meeting Framework

Your first meeting with a prospect should follow a structured discovery approach. This is not a product demo — it is a conversation designed to uncover pain, map the buying committee, and qualify the opportunity.

**Meeting structure (45 minutes):**

1. **Introduction and rapport** (5 minutes): Thank the prospect for their time. Briefly introduce yourself and ALE/Rainbow in one sentence. State the meeting objective: "I would like to understand your communications environment and challenges so I can determine if Rainbow might be a fit."

2. **Discovery questions** (25 minutes): Use persona-appropriate questions from Section 2. Listen more than you talk — the 70/30 rule (they talk 70%, you talk 30%).

3. **Preliminary alignment** (10 minutes): Summarize what you heard. Connect their pain points to Rainbow capabilities at a high level. Do not deep-dive into features yet.

4. **Next steps** (5 minutes): Propose a specific next step — a demo, a technical deep-dive, a meeting with additional stakeholders, or a reference call. Get agreement on the next step before ending the meeting.

### 5.2 Universal Discovery Questions

These questions work regardless of which persona you are meeting:

1. "What prompted you to look at communications platforms right now?"
2. "What is working well with your current setup? What is not?"
3. "Who else is involved in this evaluation?"
4. "What does your timeline look like?"
5. "Have you looked at other solutions? If so, which ones?"
6. "What would success look like six months after deployment?"
7. "Is there an allocated budget for this project?"
8. "What would happen if you did nothing — if you made no change at all?"

Question 8 is the most important. If the answer is "Nothing would change, we would be fine," there is no pain. If the answer involves risk, cost, or missed opportunity, you have a real deal.

### 5.3 Documenting Discovery

After every discovery call, document the following in your CRM:

- **MEDDIC assessment**: Score each element 0-2
- **Buying committee map**: Names, roles, attitudes
- **Pain points identified**: Specific, attributed to specific people
- **Buying signals observed**: Which of the four signals are present?
- **Competitive landscape**: Who else are they evaluating?
- **Next step agreed**: Specific action with a specific date

> **Tip:** The best salespeople spend 15 minutes after every call documenting what they learned. This discipline pays off in two ways: you never forget critical details, and you can hand off to colleagues or managers without losing context.

## Knowledge Check

**Question 1**: In a typical enterprise Rainbow deal, who is most likely to be the Economic Buyer?

- A) IT Director
- B) CIO
- C) CFO
- D) Procurement Manager

**Answer**: C — The CFO (or a senior VP with delegated budget authority) is typically the Economic Buyer in enterprise deals. While the CIO may sponsor the project and the IT Director may evaluate the technology, the CFO has the final authority to approve the budget.

---

**Question 2**: Which of the following is NOT one of the four primary buying signals for Rainbow?

- A) PBX end-of-life or maintenance expiration
- B) A competitor launching a new product
- C) RFP or RFI issued for unified communications
- D) Compliance audit or regulatory change

**Answer**: B — A competitor launching a new product is not a direct buying signal for your prospect. The four primary buying signals are: RFP/RFI issued, PBX end-of-life, compliance audit/regulatory change, and Microsoft Teams deployment or dissatisfaction.

---

**Question 3**: What does the "C" in MEDDIC stand for?

- A) Competition
- B) Criteria
- C) Champion
- D) Contract

**Answer**: C — The "C" stands for Champion — an internal advocate within the prospect's organization who actively promotes your solution and has the influence and motivation to help close the deal.

---

**Question 4**: You are meeting an IT Director for the first time. Which approach is most effective?

- A) Start with a 30-minute product demo to impress them with features
- B) Ask discovery questions and listen for pain points, using the 70/30 rule
- C) Lead with pricing to show Rainbow is cost-competitive
- D) Focus exclusively on ALE's heritage and company history

**Answer**: B — The first meeting should be focused on discovery, not presentation. Use the 70/30 rule (the prospect talks 70%, you talk 30%) to uncover pain points, understand the buying process, and build rapport. A demo and pricing discussion should come later, once you understand the prospect's specific needs.

**Question 5**: A prospect's IT Director says "We deployed Teams last year, but our Avaya PBX still runs separately and nobody wants to pay for Teams Phone licenses." What buying signal does this represent?

- A) RFP issued
- B) PBX end-of-life
- C) Compliance audit
- D) Microsoft Teams deployment with dissatisfaction

**Answer**: D — This is a classic Teams deployment gap signal. The customer adopted Teams for chat and meetings but discovered that integrating telephony (their existing PBX) with Teams is expensive and complex. This is a prime opportunity for Rainbow's PBX overlay capability, which can bridge the gap without requiring additional Teams Phone licenses.
